Live video streaming looks deceptively simple from the outside.

A camera records a match. Users open an app. The video plays.

But at Hotstar scale, that simple user experience hides one of the hardest backend problems in distributed systems: ingesting a live video feed, transforming it into multiple stream variants, delivering it to millions of users, handling unstable networks, keeping latency acceptable, and doing all of this without collapsing under bandwidth cost.

This case study walks through the design of a Hotstar-style live video streaming system, using the example of a large live sports event such as a cricket match.

The goal is not to reproduce Hotstar’s actual internal architecture. The goal is to learn the system design concepts behind large-scale live streaming.

We will cover requirements, capacity estimates, ingestion, transcoding, HLS/DASH, adaptive bitrate streaming, CDN delivery, worker scaling, latency, cost implications, failure modes, monitoring, and virtual advertising.

---

## 1. What Are We Designing?

We are designing a live video streaming platform similar to Hotstar, where millions of users can watch the same live sports event through phones, laptops, smart TVs, and web browsers.

A typical example is an India vs Pakistan cricket match where the source feed comes from a stadium or broadcast setup and millions of users stream the match in real time.

At a high level, the system must do three things:

1. **Ingest** the live video feed.
2. **Transform (Transcode)** the video into multiple resolutions, codecs, and delivery formats.
3. **Deliver** the processed video to millions of users efficiently.

The core challenge is that live streaming has two very different scaling problems:

* **The Compute Problem**: The original video must be transcoded into multiple versions (1080p, 720p, 480p, 360p) using different codecs.
* **The Bandwidth Fan-Out Problem**: Millions of users must receive the stream concurrently, and video data is massive.

In most live streaming systems, storage is not the bottleneck. **Bandwidth and real-time processing are.**

---

## 2. Functional Requirements

* **Live Event Playback**: Users should be able to open the app or website and watch a live event.
* **Multi-Device Support**: The platform should support multiple device types: mobile phones, browsers, laptops, tablets, and smart TVs.
* **Multi-Quality Stream**: The system should support multiple video qualities (1080p, 720p, 480p, 360p).
* **Adaptive Bitrate (ABR)**: The player should automatically switch quality depending on network conditions.
* **Massive Concurrency**: The platform should support millions of concurrent viewers.
* **Entitlement & Auth**: The system should allow paid/protected content, meaning only authorized users should be able to access certain streams.
* **Global Delivery**: The platform should support global or regional delivery, for example India, US, UK, and other regions.
* **Virtual Advertising**: The platform may support virtual advertising, where different user cohorts see different ads inside the live video.

---

## 3. Non-Functional Requirements

* **Scalability**: The system should scale to millions of concurrent viewers.
* **Low Buffering**: Video playback should have minimal buffering.
* **Acceptable Latency**: For normal sports streaming, 15–30 seconds of live delay is acceptable. For low-latency use cases, the target may be closer to 2–6 seconds (though this increases complexity).
* **High Availability**: If a match is live, downtime is immediately visible to users. The system must target $99.99\%$ availability.
* **Fault Tolerance**: Failure in one transcoding worker, one CDN region, or one codec pipeline should not bring down the entire stream.
* **Cost Efficiency**: The design should be cost-effective because CDN bandwidth can become extremely expensive.

## 4. Key Concepts Before Designing

Before jumping into architecture, we need to understand a few video streaming terms.

### Ingestion

Ingestion is the process of receiving the live video feed from the broadcaster or camera setup into our backend. For live events, the feed may come from stadium infrastructure, broadcast vans, production studios, or professional hardware encoders.

The ingestion protocol could be RTMP, SRT, RIST, or WebRTC ingest. In many simplified system design discussions, RTMP over TCP is used as the ingestion protocol.

### Transcoding

Transcoding means converting the original source video into different formats. For example, the source feed may be high-quality 4K or 1080p. But not every user can watch that quality due to network variations. So we generate multiple versions (1080p, 720p, 480p, 360p).

Each version may also be encoded using a codec such as H.264, H.265/HEVC, VP9, or AV1.

### Codec

A codec defines how video is compressed and decompressed:
* **H.264**: Widely supported and safe for broad compatibility.
* **H.265/HEVC**: Provides better compression ($~30-50\%$ bandwidth savings over H.264), but device support and licensing can be concerns.
* **VP9 & AV1**: Reduce bandwidth even further, but live encoding is computationally expensive and not universally supported.

A real system does not blindly generate every resolution-codec combination. It defines an **encoding ladder** based on device support, bandwidth savings, encoding cost, and product needs.

### HLS and DASH

HLS (HTTP Live Streaming) and DASH (Dynamic Adaptive Streaming over HTTP) are HTTP-based adaptive streaming protocols. Instead of sending one continuous video stream directly to the user, the video is split into small time-based segments.

For example:
```text
segment_1001.ts
segment_1002.ts
segment_1003.ts
```

A manifest file tells the player which segments exist and where to download them:
* For HLS, the manifest is usually `.m3u8`.
* For DASH, the manifest is usually `.mpd`.

The benefit of HLS/DASH is that they work over normal HTTP, which makes them highly CDN-friendly.

### Adaptive Bitrate Streaming (ABR)

Adaptive bitrate streaming means the player can switch between qualities based on network conditions:
* Good bandwidth $\rightarrow$ 1080p.
* Network drops $\rightarrow$ 480p.
* Network recovers $\rightarrow$ 720p or 1080p.

The client does this by reading the manifest and choosing the appropriate video segments.

### CDN (Content Delivery Network)

A CDN is a distributed network of edge servers. Instead of every user downloading video from our origin server, users download from nearby CDN edge locations. Without a CDN, a Hotstar-scale live event is practically impossible.

## 5. Capacity Estimation

Let us estimate the size and bandwidth requirements for one live cricket match.

### Storage Estimation

Assume the match lasts **10 hours**. A 2-hour HD movie is approximately 2 GB, so a 10-hour HD stream is approximately $10\text{ GB}$.

Now assume we generate the following renditions:
* 1080p = 10 GB
* 720p = 5 GB
* 480p = 2.5 GB
* 360p = 1.25 GB

Total for one codec:
\[10 + 5 + 2.5 + 1.25 = 18.75\text{ GB}\]

If we support 4 codecs:
\[18.75\text{ GB} \times 4 = 75\text{ GB}\]

So the processed output for one 10-hour stream is roughly **75 GB**. Storage is highly manageable.

### Bandwidth & Traffic Estimation

The real problem starts when millions of users consume the stream.

* Assume **10 million** concurrent users watch the match.
* $50\%$ watch in Full HD ($2\text{ GB}$ per 2 hours).
* $50\%$ watch in 480p ($0.5\text{ GB}$ per 2 hours).
* Assume each user watches **2 hours** on average.

$$\text{HD Users:} \quad 5,000,000 \times 2\text{ GB} = 10,000,000\text{ GB}$$
$$\text{480p Users:} \quad 5,000,000 \times 0.5\text{ GB} = 2,500,000\text{ GB}$$
$$\text{Total Data Served:} \quad 12,500,000\text{ GB} \approx 12.5\text{ PB}$$

So for just 2 hours of viewing by 10 million users, the CDN may need to serve around **12.5 Petabytes (PB)** of data.

Let us estimate the average bandwidth:
* **Full HD**: $\frac{2\text{ GB}}{2\text{ hours}} = 1\text{ GB/hour} \approx 2.22\text{ Mbps}$
* **480p**: $\frac{0.5\text{ GB}}{2\text{ hours}} = 0.25\text{ GB/hour} \approx 0.56\text{ Mbps}$
* **Weighted Average Bitrate**: $(50\% \times 2.22) + (50\% \times 0.56) = 1.39\text{ Mbps}$ per active viewer.

For 10 million concurrent viewers:
\[10,000,000 \times 1.39\text{ Mbps} = 13.9\text{ Tbps}\]

> [!IMPORTANT]
> 13.9 Terabits per second (Tbps) is an enormous amount of traffic. No normal application server layer should directly serve this. The system must rely entirely on a robust Multi-CDN network.

## 6. High-Level Architecture

The system is split into four major planes:

```text
Ingest Plane:
Camera / broadcaster → Upload service → raw segment storage

Processing Plane:
Video event queue → transcoding workers → packaged renditions

Origin and Distribution Plane:
Processed object storage → regional origins → CDN

Playback Control Plane:
Client → playback API → signed manifest URL → CDN playback
```

Below is the complete end-to-end architecture layout:

![Live Video Streaming Architecture](/images/blog/hotstar-live-video-streaming-system-design.svg)

The central idea is that **users do not receive video bytes from application servers**. Application servers only authenticate the user, check entitlement, determine device capabilities, and return a CDN playback URL. The actual video segments are delivered through the CDN.

## 7. Video Ingestion

The broadcaster sends a high-quality feed to our upload/ingestion service.

```text
Camera / production system
   ↓ RTMP/SRT
Upload service
   ↓
Raw segment storage
   ↓
Video event queue
```

The upload service has two responsibilities:
1. Persist the raw input chunks into durable storage.
2. Publish events to a queue so downstream workers can process those chunks.

An example video event:
```json
{
  "livestreamId": "ind-vs-aus-final",
  "segmentNumber": 9281,
  "inputPath": "s3://raw/ind-vs-aus/segment_9281.ts",
  "startTimestamp": "2026-06-21T10:15:06Z",
  "durationSeconds": 6
}
```

The upload service should not synchronously perform transcoding. Instead, ingestion and processing are decoupled through a message broker (e.g., Kafka).

---

## 8. What Are Video Segments?

Live video is continuous, but distributed systems work better with bounded units of work. So the live stream is split into time-based chunks or segments (typically **6 seconds**).

```text
segment_0001 = 00:00 to 00:06
segment_0002 = 00:06 to 00:12
segment_0003 = 00:12 to 00:18
```

For a 10-hour match:
$$\frac{36,000\text{ seconds}}{6\text{ seconds}} = 6,000\text{ raw segments}$$

If we generate 4 resolutions and 4 codecs:
$$4 \text{ resolutions} \times 4 \text{ codecs} = 16 \text{ output variants}$$
$$6,000 \text{ raw segments} \times 16 \text{ variants} = 96,000 \text{ transformation jobs}$$

This is why horizontal scaling matters. We do not have just one worker for 1080p and one for 720p; we have thousands of segment-level jobs continuously arriving.

---

## 9. Transcoding and Worker Scaling

A transformation job represents a single unit of work for a segment:
```json
{
  "livestreamId": "ind-vs-aus-final",
  "segmentNumber": 9281,
  "inputPath": "s3://raw/ind-vs-aus/segment_9281.ts",
  "outputResolution": "720p",
  "codec": "h264",
  "outputPath": "s3://processed/ind-vs-aus/hls/h264/720p/segment_9281.ts"
}
```

We scale the worker fleet using two models:
* **Specialized Model**: Separate worker pools for specific codecs or resolutions (e.g., AV1 workers on GPU instances, H.264 on CPU).
* **Generic Model**: Workers consume jobs from a unified queue.

A rough formula for worker count is:
$$\text{Required Workers} = \text{Jobs generated per second} \times \text{Average processing time per job}$$

* Assume one stream creates 8 output jobs every 6 seconds ($1.33\text{ jobs/sec}$).
* If each job takes 4 seconds of compute time:
\[1.33 \times 4 = 5.32\text{ workers}\]

So around 6 workers are needed just to keep up with one live stream without any safety margin.

---

## 10. Encoding Ladder: Do We Generate Every Combination?

A naive design generates every resolution for every codec. This is expensive and unnecessary. A real system defines an **encoding ladder**:

| Resolution | Bitrate (H.264) | Bitrate (HEVC) | Target Devices |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1080p** | 4.5 Mbps | 2.5 Mbps | Smart TVs, Laptops |
| **720p** | 2.2 Mbps | 1.2 Mbps | Tablets, Tablets |
| **480p** | 1.0 Mbps | 0.6 Mbps | Budget Phones |
| **360p** | 0.5 Mbps | 0.3 Mbps | Poor Network Connections |

> [!NOTE]
> H.264 is used as the baseline for maximum device compatibility. HEVC/AV1 are generated for compatible modern devices to save bandwidth, while older resolutions/codecs are skipped if their cost outweighs the optimization benefits.

## 11. Packaging and Manifests

After transcoding, the output must be packaged for HLS or DASH.

For HLS, the system produces a master playlist and media playlists:
```text
master.m3u8
  ├── 360p/playlist.m3u8
  ├── 480p/playlist.m3u8
  ├── 720p/playlist.m3u8
  └── 1080p/playlist.m3u8
```

For live video, the playlist is updated continuously using a **sliding window**:
* **At time T**: contains segments 9281, 9282, 9283.
* **At time T+6s**: contains segments 9282, 9283, 9284.

The **manifest updater** must update playlists atomically and only include segments that are completely uploaded. If a manifest points to a segment that is not yet ready, the player will throw errors or buffer.

---

## 12. Object Storage and Origin

Processed segments and manifests are stored in object storage (e.g., S3, GCS).

```text
s3://live/ind-vs-aus/hls/h264/1080p/segment_9281.ts
s3://live/ind-vs-aus/hls/h264/720p/segment_9281.ts
s3://live/ind-vs-aus/hls/master.m3u8
```

The object storage acts as the primary origin. However, client players must never fetch directly from object storage; they fetch through a CDN layer.

---

## 13. CDN Delivery

CDN is the heart of large-scale streaming:

```text
Without CDN: 10 million users → Origin Servers (Instant Collapse)
With CDN:    10 million users → Nearby CDN Edges → Origin (Only on cache miss)
```

Live sports have a huge overlap in what users watch. Millions of users request the exact same segment at the same time. The मुंबई edge fetches `segment_9281.ts` once from the origin and serves it to $100,000$ local users directly from cache.

> [!TIP]
> **CDN Caching Strategy:**
> * **Video Segments (`.ts`, `.m4s`)**: Cache aggressively (immutable).
> * **Manifests (`.m3u8`, `.mpd`)**: Cache with a very short TTL ($~1-2\text{ seconds}$) since they change continuously.

---

## 14. Regional Origins and Origin Shielding

To protect the primary origin from a stampeding herd (cache miss storm), we introduce **Origin Shields**:

```text
Client → CDN Edge → Regional Origin Shield → Primary Origin (S3)
```

If $100$ edge servers in Chennai miss a segment, they all call the Chennai Origin Shield. The Origin Shield requests the segment once from S3, caches it, and returns it to all 100 edges.

---

## 15. Playback API

The client first calls a Playback API instead of accessing the CDN directly.

```http
GET /v1/livestreams/{livestreamId}/playback
Authorization: Bearer <token>
```

The playback service performs several checks:
* Is the user authenticated?
* Do they have the entitlement to watch (e.g., Premium subscription)?
* What is their geographic region (for licensing/blackouts)?
* What device features do they support (codecs, DRM)?
* Which CDN or endpoint should they be routed to?

```json
{
  "livestreamId": "ind-vs-aus-final",
  "protocol": "hls",
  "manifestUrl": "https://cdn.example.com/live/ind-vs-aus/master.m3u8?token=...",
  "expiresAt": 1790000000,
  "drmLicenseUrl": "https://license.example.com/widevine",
  "availableRenditions": ["360p", "480p", "720p", "1080p"]
}
```

> [!NOTE]
> This is a key design principle: **Keep application servers out of the video byte path.** They only handle authorization, billing, metadata, and signed URLs.

## 16. HLS/DASH vs WebRTC

| Feature | HLS / DASH | WebRTC |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Latency** | Medium-High ($6-30\text{ seconds}$) | Ultra-Low ($<1\text{ second}$) |
| **CDN Caching** | Excellent (HTTP-based) | Poor (Requires specialized SFU/Media servers) |
| **Scalability** | Extremely High (Millions) | Moderate to High (Requires massive server grid) |
| **Egress Cost** | Low | High |

For a massive broadcast like a cricket match, HLS/DASH is the standard choice. WebRTC is reserved for interactive, low-latency features (e.g., live chat host video, real-time gaming overlay).

---

## 17. Latency Estimation

Live streaming latency is not just network transfer speed. It is a compound pipeline budget:

$$\text{Total Latency} = \text{Capture Delay} + \text{Ingestion} + \text{Segment Formation} + \text{Transcoding} + \text{Manifest Update} + \text{CDN Cache Fill} + \text{Client Buffer}$$

A realistic standard HLS/DASH latency budget:
* **Capture + Ingest**: $0.5 - 1\text{s}$
* **Segment Formation (6s segment)**: $6\text{s}$
* **Transcoding/Packaging**: $2 - 4\text{s}$
* **Storage + Manifest Update**: $0.5 - 1\text{s}$
* **CDN Cache Fill**: $0.5 - 2\text{s}$
* **Client Buffer (typically 3 segments)**: $18\text{s}$
* **Total Latency**: **$27.5 - 32\text{s}$**

To achieve Low-Latency HLS (LL-HLS) down to $2-5\text{ seconds}$, we use:
* **Shorter segments** (e.g., $1-2\text{ seconds}$) or **CMAF chunks** (partial segments).
* Reduced client playback buffers.
* Faster hardware encoders.

*Trade-off*: Shorter segments increase CDN requests and HTTP overhead by $6\text{x}$, degrading cache hit ratios and increasing cost.

## 18. Cost Implications

The biggest cost in live streaming is CDN egress.

Using our earlier calculation:
* $10\text{ million}$ users.
* Average $2\text{ hours}$ watched.
* Total egress: $12.5\text{ PB}$.

At a wholesale CDN rate of **$0.01 per GB**:
\[12,500,000\text{ GB} \times \$0.01 = \$125,000\text{ per match}\]

At standard retail cloud rates ($0.08 per GB$), this would be $1\text{ million dollars}$. Thus, maximizing CDN cache hit ratios, negotiating multi-CDN contracts, and using peerings directly affect the business survival of the streaming company.

## 19. Push vs Pull CDN

* **Pull-based CDN**: CDN edge requests content from the origin only when the first user requests it. Simple, but can cause a "cache-miss stampede" at the origin when a new segment goes live.
* **Push-based CDN (Pre-warming)**: The origin pushes segments to key regional edges immediately upon creation. Prevents origin spikes and guarantees low startup time, but wastes bandwidth if pushed to cold regions.

*Hybrid approach*: Pre-warm highly active areas (e.g., metro areas in India) and pull-cache for minor regional edges.

---

## 20. Consistency and Ordering

Adaptive bitrate switching requires segment synchronization. If a client switches from 720p to 1080p HEVC, segment boundaries must align perfectly.

Key techniques:
* **Monotonic Sequence Numbers**: Ensure segments are published sequentially.
* **Keyframe Alignment**: Every rendition must have keyframes (I-frames) at the exact same frame offsets to allow seamless switching.
* **Idempotent Workers**: Prevent duplicate jobs from writing conflicting segments.
* **Atomic Manifest Updates**: Write manifests to temporary paths and rename them atomically.

## 21. Failure Modes & Mitigations

| Failure Scenario | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ingest Pipeline Down** | No input feed | Redundant ingest paths (Primary & Backup SRT links over different ISPs). |
| **Worker Queue Backlog** | Live delay increases | Scale out transcoder instances; dynamically drop heavy/expensive codecs (e.g., AV1). |
| **Primary CDN Outage** | Millions disconnected | Multi-CDN routing with automated DNS/Anycast failover based on performance metrics. |
| **Manifest Updater Crash** | Video stream freezes | Standalone stateless updater instances with lease lock; fall back to caching last known good manifest. |

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Playback continuity is more important than quality or ad revenue.** If the personalization system or an advanced codec pipeline fails, the system must immediately fall back to a raw clean H.264 stream.

---

## 22. Graceful Degradation Strategy

During extreme traffic spikes or compute failure:
1. Drop expensive codecs first (transcode H.264 only, turn off AV1/HEVC to free up CPU).
2. Cap the maximum resolution (e.g., restrict highest quality to 720p).
3. Increase player buffer size dynamically to absorb CDN errors.
4. Disable virtual ads and fall back to the clean base broadcast.

---

## 23. Monitoring and Observability

Key metrics to track in real time:

* **Ingest**: Ingest bitrate, dropped frames, encoder CPU health.
* **Processing**: Kafka queue lag, transcoding time per segment, worker GPU utilization.
* **Distribution**: CDN cache hit ratio ($>99\%$), origin egress, 404/5xx error rates.
* **Playback (Client)**: Rebuffering ratio, video startup time (VST), average playback bitrate, drop frames, DRM license acquisition time.
* **Ad Injection**: Masking confidence, ad server response latency, campaign pacing.

## 24. Virtual Advertising for Live Sports

Virtual advertising inserts targeted ads onto dynamic surfaces (like boundary boards or stadium grass) inside the stream.

```text
Stadium Camera -> Object/Field Recognition -> User Cohort Selection -> Graphic Rendering -> Transcoding -> CDN
```

### The Personalization Scaling Problem
Personalizing video uniquely for all $10\text{ million}$ users requires generating $10\text{ million}$ different streams. This destroys CDN caching because every segment request would be a cache miss.

**The Solution: Cohort-Level Personalization.**
Instead of per-user rendering, group viewers into a controlled set of cohorts (e.g., $20-50$ categories):
* Cohort A: India | Hindi | Mobile | Sports Category.
* Cohort B: US | English | Desktop | Premium.

The player calls the Playback API, gets assigned to a cohort, and requests that cohort's manifest:
```text
/live/match/cohort_A/1080p/segment_9281.ts
```
Since millions of users belong to Cohort A, the CDN caches it effectively, preserving bandwidth economics.

---

## 25. Virtual Advertising Failure Strategy

> [!TIP]
> The virtual ad pipeline must be completely non-blocking. If ad ranking APIs time out or computer vision segmentation fails, the renderer must immediately skip overlays or fall back to the stadium's physical ad boards. The live match must continue.

## 26. Interview-Level Architecture Summary

If asked to summarize this architecture in a system design interview:

> "We ingest the live feed from the broadcaster through redundant upload servers using RTMP or SRT. The ingest service splits the stream into 6-second segments, uploads them to raw storage, and publishes events to Kafka.
>
> A horizontally scalable GPU/CPU transcoding fleet consumes these events to generate an ABR ladder of resolutions (1080p down to 360p) and codecs. The packaged output segments are written to origin storage.
>
> A manifest updater atomically updates the sliding-window `.m3u8` playlists. These assets are pulled through origin shields and distributed globally via a multi-CDN network.
>
> When a client starts playback, it queries the Playback API, which checks auth/entitlements, assigns them to one of 30 ad cohorts, and returns a signed CDN URL. This separates control planes from the media byte path, allowing us to serve millions of concurrent viewers with high cache hit ratios and low buffering."

## 27. Common Mistakes in This Design

1. **Streaming bytes from backend servers**: Running Node.js/Go APIs to send video chunk bytes. *Correction*: Use APIs only for control; let CDNs serve the bytes.
2. **Ignoring CDN economics**: Assuming cloud egress rates are fine. At scale, bandwidth costs are the primary risk factor.
3. **Personalizing per user**: Rendering custom ads for each viewer. This destroys CDN caching. Always group users into cohorts.
4. **Synchronous transcoding**: Transcoding directly inside the upload request. If a worker fails, the stream halts. Use queues to decouple.
5. **No Keyframe Alignment**: Having mismatched segment bounds across resolutions. This makes the player buffer or freeze during quality shifts.

## 28. Final Mental Model

```text
Live event source
   ↓ (RTMP/SRT)
Ingest Service
   ↓ (Segment)
Raw Segments
   ↓ (Notify)
Message Queue
   ↓
Transcoding Fleet (ABR Ladder)
   ↓ (Packaged ts chunks)
Origin Storage (GCS/S3)
   ↓ (Origin Shield)
CDN (Edge Caching)
   ↓
Client Player (ABR HLS/DASH)
```

| Component | Role |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Transcoding** | Solves the compute problem. |
| **CDN** | Solves the bandwidth problem. |
| **Queues** | Solve decoupling and backpressure. |
| **ABR** | Solves network variability. |
| **Cohorts** | Preserves CDN efficiency for targeted ads. |
| **Graceful Degradation** | Protects availability above all else. |
